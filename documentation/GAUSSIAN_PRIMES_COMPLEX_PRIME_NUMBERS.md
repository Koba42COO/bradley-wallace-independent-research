## 1. GAUSSIAN INTEGERS: THE COMPLEX EXTENSION

### 1.1 Definition

**Gaussian integers** are complex numbers of the form:
$$a + bi$$
where $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$ (integers) and $i = \sqrt{-1}$.

**Notation:** $\mathbb{Z}[i] = \{a + bi : a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}$

### 1.2 The Norm Function

The **norm** of a Gaussian integer $z = a + bi$ is:
$$N(z) = |z|^2 = a^2 + b^2$$

**Key Properties:**
- $N(z) \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ (always a positive integer)
- $N(zw) = N(z)N(w)$ (multiplicative)
- $N(z) = 1$ if and only if $z$ is a unit ($\pm 1, \pm i$)

## 2. GAUSSIAN PRIMES: COMPLEX PRIME NUMBERS

### 2.1 Definition

A **Gaussian prime** is a Gaussian integer that:
1. Is not a unit ($\pm 1, \pm i$)
2. Cannot be factored into two non-unit Gaussian integers

**Formally:** $p \in \mathbb{Z}[i]$ is Gaussian prime if:
- $p \neq 0$ and $p$ is not a unit
- If $p = ab$ for $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}[i]$, then either $a$ or $b$ is a unit

### 2.2 Classification of Gaussian Primes

**Theorem:** A Gaussian integer $z = a + bi$ is prime if and only if:

1. **$N(z)$ is a rational prime** (ordinary prime) **and** $N(z) \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$, OR
2. **$z$ is associated to** (i.e., differs by a unit from) **a rational prime** $p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$, OR
3. **$N(z) = p^2$** where $p$ is a rational prime with $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ and $z$ is not divisible by $p$

**Special Cases:**
- **$1 + i$** is Gaussian prime (norm = 2)
- **$i$** is Gaussian prime (norm = 1, but it's a unit - special case)
- **$2 + i$** is Gaussian prime (norm = 5, and 5 ≡ 1 mod 4)
- **$3$** is Gaussian prime (norm = 9, and 3 ≡ 3 mod 4)
